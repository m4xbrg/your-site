## Basic practice

1. **Direct substitution** _(Difficulty: Basic)._ Compute $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2} (3x^2 - x + 4)$ using limit laws.

   **Solution.** Substitute $x=2$: $3(2)^2 - 2 + 4 = 12 - 2 + 4 = 14$.

2. **Linear limit** _(Difficulty: Basic)._ Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to -1} (5x - 7)$.

   **Solution.** Substitute directly: $5(-1) - 7 = -5 - 7 = -12$.

## Intermediate drills

3. **Polynomial trend** _(Difficulty: Intermediate)._ Using limit laws, compute $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0} (2x^3 + 7x)$ and state why substitution is valid.

   **Solution.** Substitute $x=0$: $2(0)^3 + 7(0)=0$. Polynomials are continuous everywhere, so direct substitution matches the limit.

4. **Factor to remove a hole** _(Difficulty: Intermediate)._ Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 3} \dfrac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

   **Solution.** Factor numerator: $x^2-9=(x-3)(x+2)$. Cancel $x-3$ (for $x\neq3$) to get $\lim_{x\to3}(x+2)=5$. The original function has a removable discontinuity at $x=3$.

5. **Rationalizing radicals** _(Difficulty: Intermediate)._ Compute $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 4} \dfrac{\sqrt{x} - 2}{x - 4}$.

   **Solution.** Multiply by the conjugate: $\dfrac{\sqrt{x} - 2}{x - 4}\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{x}+2}{\sqrt{x}+2}=\dfrac{x-4}{(x-4)(\sqrt{x}+2)}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x}+2}$. Now substitute $x=4$ to get $\dfrac{1}{2+2}=\dfrac14$.

## Conceptual insights

6. **Domain and continuity** _(Difficulty: Conceptual)._ For $f(x)=\dfrac{2x^2 + 3x - 2}{x^2 - 1}$, list points where $f$ is undefined, classify the discontinuities, and state the limit at those points if it exists.

   **Solution.** Denominator zero at $x=\pm1$. Factor: $f(x)=\dfrac{(2x-1)(x+2)}{(x-1)(x+1)}$. At $x=1$, common factor? none, so vertical asymptote, limits are infinite (do not exist finitely). At $x=-1$, factor cancels? yes $(x+1)$ cancels leaving $\dfrac{(2x-1)(x+2)}{x-1}$ for $x\neq -1$, so $\lim_{x\to -1} f(x)=\dfrac{(2(-1)-1)((-1)+2)}{-1-1}=\dfrac{-3\cdot1}{-2}=\dfrac32$, removable discontinuity.

7. **Limit laws justification** _(Difficulty: Conceptual)._ Explain why the limit of a product equals the product of limits when both exist, and give a quick example using $\lim_{x\to2}(x^2-4)(x-1)$.

   **Solution.** Limit laws arise from epsilon–delta definitions: if each factor stays close to its limit, their product stays close to the product. Example: $\lim_{x\to2}(x^2-4)=0$ and $\lim_{x\to2}(x-1)=1$, so the product limit is $0\cdot1=0$; substitution confirms it.

## Curveball challenge

8. **Composite simplification** _(Difficulty: Curveball)._ Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 1} \dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 - x}$ and explain each algebraic step.

   **Solution.** Factor: numerator $(x-1)(x+1)$, denominator $x(x-1)$. Cancel $(x-1)$ (for $x\neq1$) to get $\lim_{x\to1}\dfrac{x+1}{x}$. Substitute $x=1$ to obtain $\dfrac{2}{1}=2$. The cancellation removes a hole at $x=1$, so the simplified expression captures the limit.

9. **Piecewise trap** _(Difficulty: Curveball)._ Define $g(x)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}, & x\neq2, \\ 10, & x=2.\end{cases}$ Find $\lim_{x\to2} g(x)$ and state whether $g$ is continuous at $2$.

   **Solution.** For $x\neq2$, $g(x)=x+2$. Thus $\lim_{x\to2} g(x)=4$. But $g(2)=10$, so the function jumps at $2$ and is not continuous; the hole-filled value should be $4$ to restore continuity.
