## Basic practice

1. **Identify $\delta$ for a linear function** _(Difficulty: Basic)._ Show that $\lim_{x\to 2}(3x+1)=7$ by finding a $\delta$ in terms of $\varepsilon$.

   **Solution.** $| (3x+1)-7|=3|x-2|$. Choose $\delta=\varepsilon/3$ so that $|x-2|<\delta$ implies $|3x+1-7|<\varepsilon$.

2. **Translate notation** _(Difficulty: Basic)._ Rewrite the statement “$\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=L$” using $\varepsilon$ and $\delta$ language.

   **Solution.** For every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that if $0<|x-a|<\delta$, then $|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$.

## Intermediate drills

3. **Quadratic example** _(Difficulty: Intermediate)._ Prove $\lim_{x\to1}(x^2)=1$ by selecting $\delta$ as a function of $\varepsilon$.

   **Solution.** $|x^2-1|=|x-1||x+1|$. If we restrict $|x-1|<1$, then $|x+1|<3$. Choose $\delta=\min\{1,\varepsilon/3\}$ so $|x^2-1|<3|x-1|<\varepsilon$.

4. **Absolute value function** _(Difficulty: Intermediate)._ Verify the limit $\lim_{x\to0} |x| =0$ with an explicit $\delta$.

   **Solution.** $||x|-0|=|x|$. Setting $\delta=\varepsilon$ ensures $|x|<\delta$ implies $||x|-0|<\varepsilon$.

## Conceptual insights

5. **Choosing a neighborhood** _(Difficulty: Conceptual)._ Why is it useful to bound $|x-a|$ by $1$ (or another convenient number) before picking $\delta$ in epsilon–delta proofs?

   **Solution.** Bounding $|x-a|$ controls other factors involving $x$ (like $|x+1|$) by replacing them with numerical caps, simplifying the relationship between $\varepsilon$ and $\delta$.

6. **Graphical meaning** _(Difficulty: Conceptual)._ Describe the geometric picture of the $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ definition around a point $(a,L)$.

   **Solution.** An $\varepsilon$-band forms a horizontal strip around $L$. A suitable $\delta$ selects a vertical window around $a$ so that the graph of $f$ inside this window stays within the strip, capturing closeness in both $x$ and $y$.

## Curveball challenge

7. **Composite function** _(Difficulty: Curveball)._ Using epsilon–delta reasoning, show that if $\lim_{x\to a} f(x)=L$ and $g$ is continuous at $L$, then $\lim_{x\to a} g(f(x))=g(L)$.

   **Solution.** Given $\varepsilon>0$, continuity of $g$ at $L$ gives $\eta>0$ with $|y-L|<\eta \Rightarrow |g(y)-g(L)|<\varepsilon$. Since $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$, choose $\delta$ so $|x-a|<\delta$ implies $|f(x)-L|<\eta$. Then $|g(f(x))-g(L)|<\varepsilon$, proving the limit.

8. **Bounding a rational function** _(Difficulty: Curveball)._ Prove $\lim_{x\to2} \dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}=4$ using an explicit $\delta$ that handles the denominator.

   **Solution.** Simplify for $x\neq2$: $\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}=x+2$. Choose $|x-2|<1$ so $1<x<3$ and $|x+2|<5$. To make $|x+2-4|=|x-2|<\varepsilon$, set $\delta=\min\{1,\varepsilon\}$. Then $|x-2|<\delta$ ensures the difference is below $\varepsilon$.
