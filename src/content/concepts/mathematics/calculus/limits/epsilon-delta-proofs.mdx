## Snapshot

Basic epsilon–delta proofs verify a target limit by deriving a bound that ties
output error to input proximity.

Target shape:
$$
|f(x) - L| \le C\,|x - a|\quad \Rightarrow\quad \delta = \varepsilon/C.
$$

## Formal Statement

To prove
$$
\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L,
$$
show that
$$
\forall \varepsilon>0,\ \exists \delta>0:\ 0<|x-a|<\delta \ \Rightarrow\ |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon.
$$

For linear $f(x)=mx+b$:
$$
|f(x) - (ma+b)| = |m|\,|x-a| \quad \Rightarrow\quad \delta = \varepsilon/|m| \ (m\neq 0).
$$
For constant functions, any $\delta>0$ works.

## Intuition & Interpretation

Template:

1. **Rewrite $|f(x)-L|$** to expose a factor of $|x-a|$.  
2. **Bound auxiliary factors** (e.g., $|x+1|$ near $x=1$).  
3. **Solve for $\delta$** in $C|x-a|<\varepsilon$.  
4. **Verify** that $0<|x-a|<\delta$ implies $|f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$.

## Examples & Non-Examples

### Examples

1. **Linear**
$$
\lim_{x\to2} (3x+1) = 7,\quad |(3x+1)-7|=3|x-2|,\quad \delta=\varepsilon/3.
$$

2. **Constant**
$$
|5-5|=0<\varepsilon \quad\Rightarrow\quad \delta\ \text{arbitrary}.
$$

3. **Quadratic via bounding**
$$
|x^2-1| = |x-1||x+1|.
$$
If $|x-1|<1$, then $x\in(0,2)$ so $|x+1|<3$; hence
$$
|x^2-1| < 3|x-1|,\quad \delta=\min\{1,\varepsilon/3\}.
$$

### Non-Examples

1. Non‑finite limits (definition proves only finite limits).  
2. Oscillatory examples like $\sin(1/x)$ near $0$ (no single $\delta$ works).  
3. Circular reasoning (asserting the implication before deriving $\delta$).

## Properties & Relationships

- Linear: direct factoring.  
- Polynomial: bound auxiliary factors.  
- Builds rigorous foundations for limit laws and continuity.

## Common Pitfalls

1. Choosing $\delta$ before deriving the relationship.  
2. Including $x=a$ in the inequality.  
3. Failing to bound auxiliary expressions.

## Exercises

1. **Linear proof** — Prove
$$
\lim_{x\to -1} (4x-2) = -6.
$$

2. **Quadratic with bounding** — Prove
$$
\lim_{x\to 2} x^2 = 4.
$$

3. **Delta derivation** — If $|f(x)-L|\le 7|x-a|$, what $\delta$ works for a given $\varepsilon$?

4. **Mini-proof (Sketch)** — Explain why any constant function satisfies the definition at every point.
